Steps to Effective Cancer Control Planning
Assess program priorities
 

Effective cancer control planning uses data to ensure that limited resources are directed to areas of greatest need and support efforts with the highest probability of success.

Needs Assessment
Complete a needs assessment by analyzing the cancer burden and risk factor prevalence in your area, to help identify high-risk populations. Data on needs and gaps help determine whether existing programs should be redistributed and what kinds of new programs may need to be developed.

State Cancer Profiles
The State Cancer Profiles web site provides state and county level statistical data in a variety of visual formats. At the county level, you can find:

  • Cancer Screening and Risk Factors by State
  • Summary Report for a Cancer Site
  • Visualize statistics on cancer, behavioral risk factors, and demographics for States or for counties within a State
  • Historical trends for all cancer sites
   
Identify potential partners
 

Part of the objective-setting process is performing a capacity assessment -- an analysis of existing facilities, programs, and services. One particular goal is to ensure that you identify existing cancer control components and programs early, to facilitate their subsequent coordination and integration.

Compiling, maintaining, and updating such an inventory is a formidable task. An effort is currently underway by the American Cancer Society (ACS) to create an interactive database, called the Community Assessment e-Tool. Currently, the information on the e-Tool can be accessed by contacting your local Division of the American Cancer Society. A listing of Cancer Control Regional Planners is provided to help you.

ACS and NCI are working together to develop a web-based version of the e-Tool for the next generation of the PLANET.

   
Determine effectiveness of different intervention approaches
 

Examine the science on intervention strategies from state-of-the-art medical and behavioral science. Strategies generally comprise well-defined program components or interventions but also may involve infrastructure or system improvements or development of new data.

The CDC publishes a Guide to Community Preventive Services containing the latest synthesis of the science examining various strategies. The Community Guide is periodically updated as new studies become available and analysis is completed. Currently there are chapters on Tobacco and Physical Activity. The cancer chapter will become available in 2003.
   
Find examples of research-tested intervention programs and products
 

Plan feasible strategies to address the objectives identified so far. This is the tactical plan to accompany the strategic vision. No cancer budget can possibly support all that needs doing in the area of cancer prevention and control. You must follow a systematic priority-setting process to address the various societal, political and economic considerations.

The Research-tested Programs "store of knowledge" is populated with programs that have developed out of scientifically-based studies and have been shown to be effective. The database is organized to make it easy to find and compare various intervention programs that address your main areas of interest, be they a particular cancer site, a demographic, a delivery setting, or other concerns. For many of these programs, you can also download all the program components and use them locally.
   
Plan and evaluate your program
 

Information on cancer control planning and evaluation are available at the following sites:

  • The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention have developed Guidance for Comprehensive Cancer Control Planning, which presents guidelines for developing a comprehensive cancer control (CCC) plan that can be both implemented and evaluated.
  • Contact information for comprehensive cancer control planning in your state is available through the Center for Disease Control and Prevention.
  • Other planning documents specific to different parts of the cancer control continuum (e.g. tobacco control) are also available on the topic specific pages
